Chapter 2: Inference using t-Distributions

Bumpus sparrows

case0201 <- sleuth.read('ascii/case0201.csv') # read the data
59 live, stressed sparrows were collected after a storm. Some survived and some died soon after. (Don't ask me how they were collected). Here is the number of birds in each condition: 
table(case0201$status)
## 
## PERISHED SURVIVED 
##       24       35
The length of each bird's humerus was measured. 
qplot(status, humerus, data=case0201, geom="boxplot") + coord_flip()

The two groups have about the same spread. Here is a t-test for difference between group means, assuming equal variances:

(bt <- t.test(humerus ~ status, data=case0201, var.equal=T))
## 
##  Two Sample t-test
## 
## data:  humerus by status
## t = -1.777, df = 57, p-value = 0.0809
## alternative hypothesis: true difference in means is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  -21.446   1.279
## sample estimates:
## mean in group PERISHED mean in group SURVIVED 
##                  727.9                  738.0

There is no convincing evidence of a difference (p.val = 0.0809), and removing the outliers would not increase the significance either.

Schizophrenia in Identical Twins

case0202 <- read.csv('ascii/case0202.csv')

The researchers found 15 pairs of identical twins, in which one twin was affected by schizophrenia and the other was not.

The volume of a certain brain part (?) was measured in each twin.

In almost all pairs, the unaffected twin had higher volume: 
qplot(UNAFFECT - AFFECTED, data=case0202, binwidth=.125)
Here is a paired t-test: 
(test <- with(case0202, t.test(UNAFFECT, AFFECTED, paired=T)))
## 
##  Paired t-test
## 
## data:  UNAFFECT and AFFECTED
## t = 3.229, df = 14, p-value = 0.006062
## alternative hypothesis: true difference in means is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  0.0667 0.3306
## sample estimates:
## mean of the differences 
##                  0.1987

There is a significant difference (p-val = 0.0061) in brain volume, with an average difference of 0.1987. The 95% confidence interval for the difference is 0.0667 to 0.3306.

Computational Exercises

12. Marijuana during pregnancy

diff <- 280 # users - nonusers (birth weight)
se.diff <- 46.66
deg.free <- 1095
t.stat <- (diff - 0) / se.diff
  1. 95% confidence interval for the difference between means: 
    half.interval <- qt(.975,deg.free) * se.diff
c(-half.interval, half.interval) + diff
    ## [1] 188.4 371.6
  2. 90% confidence interval for the difference between means: 
    half.interval <- qt(.95,deg.free) * se.diff
c(-half.interval, half.interval) + diff
    ## [1] 203.2 356.8
  3. 2-sided p-value for null hypothesis: 
    2 * pt(-t.stat, deg.free)
    ## [1] 2.665e-09

13. Fish Oil and Blood Pressure

ex0213 <- read.csv('ascii/ex0112.csv')
  1. Group averages of BP: 
    (aves <- with(ex0213, by(BP, DIET, FUN=mean)))
    ## DIET: fishoil
## [1] 6.571
## -------------------------------------------------------- 
## DIET: regularoil
## [1] -1.143
  2. pooled estimate of standard deviation: 
    (sizes <- table(ex0213$DIET))                # the group sizes
    ## 
##    fishoil regularoil 
##          7          7

    (sds <- with(ex0213, by(BP, DIET, FUN=sd)))  # the group standard deviations
    ## DIET: fishoil
## [1] 5.855
## -------------------------------------------------------- 
## DIET: regularoil
## [1] 3.185

    (sp <- sqrt(sum((sizes - 1) * sds^2) / (sum(sizes -1)))) # the pooled standard deviation
    ## [1] 4.713
  3. SE(mean1 - mean2): 
    (se <- sp * sqrt(sum(1/sizes)))
    ## [1] 2.519
  4. d.f. associated with sp is 12. The 97.5-th percentile of the t-dist with this degree of freedom is 2.1788.

Data Problems

21. Bumpus sparrow weight

ex0221 <- sleuth.read('ascii/ex0221.csv')
We have the weights of 59 sparrows, and whether they survived or not: 
head(ex0221)
##   weight   status
## 1   24.5 survived
## 2   26.9 survived
## 3   26.9 survived
## 4   24.3 survived
## 5   24.1 survived
## 6   26.5 survived
Group sizes, boxplot, and t-test: 
summary(ex0221$status)
## perished survived 
##       24       35

qplot(status, weight, data=ex0221, geom='boxplot') + geom_point()
t.test(weight ~status, data=ex0221)
## 
##  Welch Two Sample t-test
## 
## data:  weight by status
## t = 2.205, df = 44.3, p-value = 0.03266
## alternative hypothesis: true difference in means is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  0.07011 1.55417
## sample estimates:
## mean in group perished mean in group survived 
##                  26.28                  25.46
T-test without "outlier": 
heaviest <- which.max(ex0221$weight)
t.test(weight ~status, data=ex0221[-heaviest,])
## 
##  Welch Two Sample t-test
## 
## data:  weight by status
## t = 1.946, df = 52.05, p-value = 0.05707
## alternative hypothesis: true difference in means is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  -0.01877  1.22349
## sample estimates:
## mean in group perished mean in group survived 
##                  26.07                  25.46

Whether the difference is "significant" (at the 0.05 level) depends on that one data point, so it would be a very weak conclusion.

23. Speed limits and traffic fatalities

ex0223 <- read.csv('ascii/ex0223.csv')
For each state, we have whether it raised its speed limit (over 55) in 1996, and the percentage change in traffic fatalities from 1995 to 1996. 
head(ex0223)
##               STATE INCREASE FATALITIESCHANGE
## 1            Alaska       No            -29.0
## 2       Connecticut       No             -4.4
## 3 Dist. of Columbia       No            -80.0
## 4            Hawaii       No            -25.0
## 5           Indiana       No            -13.2
## 6          Kentucky       No              3.4

(table(ex0223$INCREASE))
## 
##  No Yes 
##  19  32

qplot(FATALITIESCHANGE, data=ex0223, binwidth=10) + facet_grid(facets=INCREASE ~ .)
So, we have most of the states increasing their speed limit, and the change in fatalities in seems to be higher among states that increased their speed limit. Yes, we have a significant t-test: 
(tt <- t.test(FATALITIESCHANGE ~ INCREASE, data=ex0223))
## 
##  Welch Two Sample t-test
## 
## data:  FATALITIESCHANGE by INCREASE
## t = -2.772, df = 28.25, p-value = 0.009747
## alternative hypothesis: true difference in means is not equal to 0
## 95 percent confidence interval:
##  -38.800  -5.833
## sample estimates:
##  mean in group No mean in group Yes 
##            -8.563            13.753
The confidence interval is (-38.7995, -5.833).
